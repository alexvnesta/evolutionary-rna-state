# Choosing and fine-tuning an encoder for the evolutionary RNA-state model

## Bottom line

For a melanoma-only ICB cohort of roughly 150 pretreatment samples on CPU-only hardware, the evidence points away from fine-tuning a raw-sequence genomic language model as the primary encoder and toward a staged strategy: quantify the six RNA phenotypes with dedicated tools, run the interpretable latent-state backbone that already exists, and treat a genomic/RNA language model as a *frozen feature extractor* first — reserving parameter-efficient fine-tuning for the one place it is defensible (mapping pretrained embeddings onto the phenotype axes) and only once a GPU host is available. This is not a counsel of caution for its own sake: it is what the two most recent independent benchmarks of DNA language models actually show, and it protects the falsifiable core of the thesis from the small-*n* overfitting trap that would sink it for the wrong reason.

## What a raw-read encoder buys

The thesis motivates a raw-read encoder because annotation-based pipelines discard the non-reference signal — splicing, intron retention, editing, transposable-element (TE) activation, fusions, cryptic ORFs — where the evolutionary RNA-state fingerprint is hypothesized to live. A model that reads nucleotides directly could in principle recover all of it without committing to any one annotation. That is the genuine appeal. The question the recent literature answers is whether current pretrained models deliver on it in the regime we are actually in.

Two 2025 benchmarks are decisive here because they were built specifically to stress-test the optimistic early claims on harder tasks and stronger baselines. The representational-power study ([Tang et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13059-025-03674-8)) evaluated frozen embeddings from DNABERT-2, HyenaDNA, Nucleotide Transformer, and GPN across regulatory tasks including alternative-splicing prediction, and found that a simple convolutional network trained on one-hot sequence *modestly outperformed* the language-model embeddings on most tasks, with embeddings also trailing supervised models such as Sei and even a LASSO baseline on Enformer features. The independent benchmark of [Feng et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-65823-8) reached a compatible conclusion across genomic and genetic tasks: no pretrained DNA foundation model was consistently strong, and gains over conventional baselines were task-dependent and often absent. The genomic-language-model review that frames both results is explicit that these disappointments are measured on *frozen* embeddings, and that whether full fine-tuning closes the gap is still open ([Benegas et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tig.2024.11.013)).

Three findings from these papers bear directly on our design choices rather than on the field in the abstract. First, **architecture size is not the axis that helps**: Tang et al. report that HyenaDNA, a lightweight state-space model, performed comparably to or better than substantially larger foundation models across their tasks. That is consistent with the sibling review's identification of Caduceus and HyenaDNA as the architecturally appropriate backbones, and an argument against reaching for the largest available transformer. Second, **the low-data regime is exactly where embeddings might earn their place**: Tang et al. tested this deliberately by down-sampling training data and found that pretrained embeddings become more competitive with one-hot encoding as labelled examples become scarce, because a large supervised model trained from scratch on few examples overfits. Our ~150-sample cohort sits in that regime, which is the strongest single argument *for* using a pretrained encoder, though as a frozen feature source feeding a small downstream model rather than a network we retrain end to end. Third, **fine-tuning, where it has worked, worked on tasks with abundant labels**: the review's positive examples (SegmentNT for genome annotation, AgroNT fine-tuned across plant traits, Evo fine-tuned to design CRISPR-Cas systems) are all settings with large task-specific datasets, not 150-patient clinical cohorts.

## Which encoder, if we use one

The choice is narrower than the field's length suggests. Attention-based models (DNABERT, [Ji et al. 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/bioinformatics/btab083); Nucleotide Transformer, [Dalla-Torre et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41592-024-02523-z)) carry a quadratic context limit that is a poor fit for splicing and TE signals spread over long spans, and they were the weaker performers in Tang et al. State-space and long-convolution models are the better match: HyenaDNA reaches single-nucleotide resolution at up to ~1 Mb context, and Caduceus adds the reverse-complement equivariance that DNA and RNA specifically require. Given that Tang et al. found HyenaDNA competitive with larger models despite its size, and that it runs at far lower cost, **HyenaDNA or Caduceus is the defensible backbone**, used frozen. Enformer ([Avsec et al. 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41592-021-01252-x)) remains a strong *supervised* feature source in the same benchmark and is worth keeping as a comparator rather than a fine-tuning target.

A second family is worth naming because it may fit the endpoint better than any DNA model: expression/transcriptome foundation models. scGPT ([Cui et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41592-024-02201-0)) and Geneformer ([Theodoris et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-023-06139-9)) are pretrained on single-cell expression and are designed for exactly the transfer-learning-into-small-cohort setting we face, and a recent bulk-oriented transcription foundation model extends this to human cell types ([Fu et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08391-z)). These operate on expression matrices rather than raw reads, so they do not directly capture the non-canonical signal the thesis is about. They are, however, the natural encoder for the interpretable-feature arm and a strong baseline the raw-read approach must beat to justify itself.

## The fine-tuning decision

Full fine-tuning or continued pretraining of a genomic LM is not feasible on the current CPU-only Mac and is not warranted at *n* ≈ 150 regardless of hardware; the benchmarks predict it would overfit and lose to the existing PCA backbone. Frozen-embedding use is feasible now and is the correct first experiment: extract HyenaDNA/Caduceus embeddings for each sample, feed them into the existing leakage-guarded latent-state and response models, and measure whether they add anything over the de-novo phenotype block. If — and only if — frozen embeddings show signal that a linear head underuses, the defensible next step is **parameter-efficient fine-tuning** (LoRA or adapters), which freezes the backbone and trains a small number of new parameters, keeping the effective parameter count matched to the sample size. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning of a *protein* language model has been shown to boost downstream predictions across diverse tasks while avoiding full-model retraining ([Schmirler et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-51844-2)), which is the closest published template for what a small-cohort RNA fine-tune should look like. It requires a GPU host we do not yet have.

The scientifically novel target for any fine-tuning is not "predict response" (that is the small-*n* trap) but "map frozen embeddings onto the six phenotype axes," turning the encoder into a learned quantifier of the evolutionary RNA state that the interpretable model then tests against response. That framing keeps the encoder's training signal (phenotype reconstruction) separate from the endpoint (ICB response), which is precisely the separation the falsifiable claims require.

## What still needs dedicated tools regardless of the encoder

An encoder does not remove the need to quantify the phenotypes explicitly, both as interpretable features and as fine-tuning targets. TE and endogenous-retrovirus expression has an established quantification path: Telescope reassigns ambiguous reads to specific TE loci ([Bendall et al. 2019](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1006453)), and its relevance to immunotherapy is not hypothetical: TE expression in tumors is associated with immune signaling and antigenicity ([Kong et al. 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-13035-2)). Splicing-derived neoantigens have a purpose-built discovery tool in SNAF, which also demonstrates that aberrant-splicing antigens are shared across patients ([Li et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1126/scitranslmed.ade2886)), directly relevant given that the harmonized iAtlas data already carry SPLICE, ERV, and FUSION neoantigen categories. And the melanoma-ICB modeling precedent for the endpoint itself is the integrative Liu et al. cohort and analysis ([Liu et al. 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-019-0654-5)), one of the variant-calls cohorts already in the registry.

## Recommendation

Proceed in this order. Build the de-novo phenotype matrices with dedicated tools (Telescope for TE/ERV, SNAF-style splicing quantification, plus editing/fusion/intron-retention measures) and run the existing interpretable backbone — this carries the falsifiable co-variation and response-organization tests on its own and needs no GPU. In parallel, evaluate HyenaDNA/Caduceus embeddings *frozen* as an additional feature block and as a comparator, since the low-data regime is where they are most likely to help. Defer any fine-tuning until (a) frozen embeddings demonstrate unused signal and (b) a GPU host is available, and when it happens, make it parameter-efficient and aim it at phenotype reconstruction rather than response. This sequence matches what the current benchmarks support, keeps every step feasible on present hardware, and reserves the compute-hungry, overfitting-prone move for the point where the evidence says it could pay off.
